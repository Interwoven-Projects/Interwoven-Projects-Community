# How Interwoven Projects Works

This document expands on the quick overview in the [main README](../README.md).

## The Five Stages

### 1. Spark (Idea)
Anyone can have an idea that sits at the intersection of two or more fields.

- Post it casually on X or in Discussions first if you want early feedback.
- When you're ready for serious collaboration, open a **Project Proposal** using the form.

### 2. Proposal
A structured issue that includes:
- Disciplines touched
- Goals and definition of success
- Roles needed
- Initial credit thoughts

The proposal becomes the public seed of the project.

### 3. Discussion & Team Formation
This is where the magic happens.

- People read the proposal.
- Interested contributors comment or open "Join Project" issues.
- Scope is clarified, roles are negotiated, and a small founding team emerges.
- A Project Lead (often the originator) usually steps forward.

**Discussion** — Most chat sessions are held on the X platform at the moment. You can also join the Discord channel here: https://discord.gg/4QRR2y3QR

Not every proposal becomes active — that's okay. Many great ideas incubate here.

### 4. Active Project
When a committed small team exists:

- The project gets an entry under `/projects/`
- A GitHub Project card moves to "Active"
- Work is broken down (issues, tasks, or simple status updates)
- Contributors record progress using **Contribution Update** issues

Active projects can live entirely in this repo or spin out to their own repositories (recommended for larger code or data projects).

### 5. Credit, Completion, and Legacy
When the main work is done:

- Final CONTRIBUTORS table / section is written (or updated)
- Results are shared (paper, tool, post, exhibition, code release, etc.)
- The project is marked completed
- Everything remains discoverable for future researchers or collaborators

Some projects naturally evolve into new proposals.

## What Makes a Good Proposal?

- Clear intersection of at least two disciplines
- Specific enough that someone can imagine contributing
- Honest about time and scope
- Open to evolution (great projects often change shape)

## What Makes a Good Contributor?

- Clear about what they can actually deliver
- Proactive about recording their work (Contribution Updates)
- Generous with credit to others
- Communicates when availability changes

## Common Questions

**Do I need to be an expert?**  
No. Thoughtful amateurs and hobbyists are often the best bridges between fields.

**Can a project be just one person?**  
Technically yes, but the spirit of Interwoven is collaboration. Solo projects are better suited to personal repositories.

**What if my project is mostly one field with a little bit of another?**  
Still welcome! The "interwoven" bar is not extremely high — meaningful cross-pollination is enough.

**How formal is the credit system?**  
Formal enough to be trustworthy, casual enough that maintaining it doesn't become a burden. The Contribution Update form exists precisely to make it easy.
